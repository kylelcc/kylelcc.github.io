My First Machine Learning Project – Kaggle Titanic Challenge

How I went from raw CSV files to predictive insights, and what I learned along the way.

Python Data Analytics Kaggle

This week I finished my first Kaggle machine learning project: the Titanic Survival Prediction challenge. It might be the "hello world" of data science, but for me it was a hands-on crash course in data cleaning, feature engineering, and model training.

The Tools I Used

I worked in Python using Pandas for data manipulation, scikit-learn for model building, and Matplotlib/Seaborn for visual exploration. I tried out Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting — and discovered that tuning hyperparameters can sometimes feel like adjusting the sails in unpredictable wind.

Key Learnings

  • Data cleaning is 80% of the job — Handling missing ages and embarked ports taught me that messy data is the rule, not the exception.
  • Feature engineering matters — Simple changes like grouping passengers by family size improved accuracy more than I expected.
  • Model selection is iterative — There’s rarely a “best” model on the first try. Testing and comparing is the only way to know.

Applying It Beyond Kaggle

The steps I followed here — understanding the data, cleaning it, finding meaningful variables, and testing hypotheses — are exactly the same skills I use in marketing analytics. Whether predicting churn, segmenting audiences, or optimizing campaigns, the core process is transferable. The Titanic project reminded me that analytics isn’t just about the “what,” but also the “why” behind the numbers.

My Next Step

Next, I plan to take on a dataset closer to my domain expertise: customer transaction data. The goal? Combine my marketing background with new ML skills to uncover patterns that humans might miss — and turn them into business value.

Have you tried the Titanic challenge? What was your biggest takeaway? Share your experience — I’d love to compare notes.

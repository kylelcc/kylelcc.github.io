Machine Learning: the Tech That Powers Modern LLMs

Understanding the ML foundations that make generative AI possible

Machine Learning Generative AI LLMs

For my learning this week, I decided to answer the common question in the field of AI: how machine learning (ML) and generative AI (GenAI) are connected, and why a strong grasp of ML fundamentals is crucial for understanding modern large language models (LLMs).

The Journey Begins: AI vs. Machine Learning

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the broad discipline—like physics in science—focused on building systems that can reason, learn, and act autonomously.

Machine Learning is a subset of AI—focused on teaching machines to learn patterns from data without explicit programming.

This “subset” perspective is important because LLMs like Gemini, GPT, or LaMDA are not magic—they’re built on the same ML principles I first learned when working with supervised and unsupervised models.

Machine Learning Basics That Power LLMs

In ML, models learn from data:

  • Supervised Learning: Train on labeled examples (e.g., bill amount → tip prediction).
  • Unsupervised Learning: Cluster or group unlabeled data to discover hidden patterns.
  • Semi-supervised Learning: A mix of labeled and unlabeled data—common in large-scale AI systems because labeled data is expensive to produce at scale.
  • Deep Learning: Uses artificial neural networks with many layers to extract complex patterns—key for LLMs.

From Discriminative to Generative Models

Discriminative Models learn the boundary between classes, while Generative Models learn the joint probability of inputs and outputs, enabling them to create new data. LLMs fall into the generative category.

Generative AI and Large Language Models

Generative AI uses massive foundation models trained on vast amounts of multimodal data. Transformers, introduced in 2018, revolutionized LLM design with attention mechanisms and encoder-decoder architectures.

Why Learning ML Fundamentals Matters for LLMs

Feature engineering and probability awareness are crucial when working with LLMs—these concepts help shape model behavior beyond trial-and-error prompting.

ML Principles in Practice

ML Principle Regular Prompt Engineering ML-Informed Prompting Why It Works Better
Feature Engineering “Summarize this market research report.” “Summarize this market research report on electric vehicle adoption for an audience of city transportation planners, focusing only on consumer behavior trends and infrastructure challenges mentioned in sections 3 and 4.” Strips away irrelevant “features” and narrows the model’s attention to the most useful parts of the data, reducing noise and improving relevance.
Probability Awareness “Give me five app ideas for food delivery.” “Using a higher temperature setting (0.85), give me five unconventional food delivery app ideas targeted at rural communities, prioritizing novelty over feasibility.” Adjusts the model’s sampling distribution to increase creativity and variety—using randomness intentionally rather than relying on default settings.

By applying ML fundamentals like feature engineering and probability awareness, you move from being a casual user to a practitioner capable of producing consistent, tailored, high-quality results with LLMs.
